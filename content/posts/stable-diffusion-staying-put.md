Can we believe anything we see online anymore?

![Trump picking up Coconuts in Thailand – AI generated](/images/stable-diffusion-trump.png)

An algorithm generated the picture shown above. The setup took only 10 minutes, and nearly anything imaginable can be generated in some form. This raises crucial questions about digital authenticity going forward—how will people distinguish real content from artificial creations?

As capability gaps widen, vulnerable populations may face sophisticated misinformation campaigns affecting them politically, socially, and financially. However, resistance to new technologies is historically common. When photography emerged, painters protested similarly; printing press opponents included bookmakers.

[Stable diffusion](https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2022/09/with-stable-diffusion-you-may-never-believe-what-you-see-online-again/) demonstrates remarkable creative potential, with examples available on [Twitter](https://mobile.twitter.com/stablediff).

## Thoughts

I reference *The Lincoln Highway* by Amor Towles, noting an observation that "the will to stay put stems not from virtues but from vices"—gluttony, sloth, greed, pride, and envy all encourage remaining geographically stable. I resonate with this perspective.

## Success and Failure

A striking asymmetry exists: success follows relatively few paths, yet failure emerges from countless variations. This mirrors Tolstoy's observation about happy versus unhappy families. Consequently, successful people often follow similar trajectories: education, employment, savings, retirement. Truly exceptional successes frequently represent outliers departing from conventional paths.
