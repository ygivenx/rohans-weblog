I reflect on a realization that despite high work volume, minimal meaningful progress occurred. This prompted recollection of Cal Newport's essay advocating for reduced workload intensity.

## Core Argument

Drawing from Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow*, the piece suggests that excessive tasks force cognitive shortcuts incompatible with quality work. Scientific and technological fields require System 2 thinking—deliberate, analytical cognition demanding sustained mental effort. High work volumes undermine this requirement.

I question whether this principle applies uniformly, noting professions like policing rely on rapid System 1 judgments. Whether increased volume degrades such thin-slicing abilities remains open.

## Practical Philosophy

The concept extends beyond professional contexts. In our attention-fragmented world—emails, content, entertainment—intentional presence matters. Focusing entirely on immediate activities (cooking without distraction, allowing others to wait) imbues tasks with meaning. Multitasking undermines this engagement.
